Inspiring and motivating others

By: Kimberly Dunham - 02/23/2017



For the first time in history there are five generations in the workforce at the same time. This means there are differences in values and work ethics. So, with multiple generations in the workforce how do you inspire and motivate your employees?

First, you need to acknowledge that one size does not fit all. What motivates the veterans or traditionalists and the baby boomers does not inspire or motivate the millennials. Generation Y or Z workers in the workplace.

Motivation is key to individual performance, group productivity, and maintaining a winning culture. Inspiration is a lost art and comes in small doses and is usually doled out in the form of praise and/or promotion.

Employees, regardless of generational placement, are more motivated when they feel needed, appreciated, and valued by their company and their peers.


Tips for engaging your multigenerational workforce:
  • 1. Create project/function oriented assignments allowing for cross-generational input.
  • 2. Communicate effectively and often using different mediums.
  • 3. Listen to your audience
  • 4. Keep an open mind when they bring issues to the table
  • 5. Look beyond appearances
  • 6. Consider individual needs
  • 7. Emphasize commonalities and strengths
  • 8. Acknowledge work/life expectations

Lastly, encourage everyone to be their best selves. Take a genuine interest in them as individuals. Talk to them about their hopes, dreams and interests. Encourage them to change jobs or seek other opportunities within the organization that are better suited to their passion and skill set.

Each generation reacts differently to motivational techniques and inspiration. The bottom line is everyone wants to be treated fairly, have their needs met and feel as though their work is making a difference no matter what that might look like in the end.

I understand that inspiring and motivating people can be difficult and tricky but it is not impossible. Having to keep 10 children that span 3 generations inspired and motivated to get up for school every morning has been nothing short of miraculous. What worked for the boys definitely did not work for the girls and vice versa. My older children were motivated to keep Mom happy and amenable to their requests for privileges when asked whereas the younger kids are not motivated to keep Mom happy; they are more interested in keeping themselves happy. One tactic to inspire my oldest daughter to excel and work extra hard at something does not phase my youngest daughter. Each has their own set of values and needs they want met and are willing to do different things to achieve the same outcome. I marvel at the ingenuity of these children and the nuances of each but am proud of how they are exceling in life and school and are motivated and inspiring individuals.

Championing Change

By: Kimberly Dunham - 02/20/2017



A champion is a person who voluntarily takes extraordinary interest in the adoption, implementation, and success of a cause, policy, program, project, or product. To have done so successfully is said to champion change. They will typically try to force the idea through deep-rooted internal resistance to change, and will evangelize it throughout the organization. This has been known to work but it has also been known to backfire.

Smart leaders are said to champion change. How? By selling their ideas; i.e., they look for ways to get people emotionally committed to the change. And, they are sure to have an edge or stake in the change. They tell stories, paint a vision of a better future, and engage positive emotions. They stay focused on the benefits rather than the costs and understand people need time to adjust to and to accept the change. They work to inspire buy-in instead of compliance.

Successful leaders understand that people in their organization do the work, change behaviors, and, ultimately, make the change happen. Championing them on will increase the chances of the change taking place and working for the good of the people and the organization.

Dr. Aubrey Daniels, noted behavioral analyst and author of Bringing Out the Best in People, makes two great comments regarding the process of change acceptance:

“People don’t resist change, they resist being changed,” and “People don’t resist change if the change provides immediate positive consequences to them.”

Smart leaders know that people are generally more willing to do things that bring personal benefit than they are to do things which benefit the organization. Over the course of time, successful leaders have learned people need both information about the reason behind the change and time to adjust to it. They also realize they can’t wait endlessly to get everyone to commit to the change. By breaking big changes into small pieces, people are more likely to accept the change more quickly. Breaking change into small pieces, helps leaders move their organizations with frequent, continual, and steady forward progress rather than through periodic big jumps. This helps build momentum and keep it moving.

I saw this work first hand in my life as I transitioned from a stay at home mom to a working mom. Having to get 10 children to adjust to me leaving the house for hours at a time was not something I was willing to thrust upon them all at once. I broke it down into bite size chunks they could get used to and successfully navigated myself back into the corporate world. This was accomplished by taking part-time work for 2-4 hours a day during which time I scheduled play dates for the kids. While they felt they were the beneficiaries of mommy being out of the house consistently while they had play dates, I was able to establish a routine in which we could both benefit from.

I negotiated with the older children routines for the younger kids by offering them a “what’s in it for me scenario”. They wanted freedom to drive, hang out with friends and be independent. They earned these privileges by assisting me with carpooling activities, giving baths and entertaining their siblings while I worked, cooked dinner and got everyone settled for the night. We worked in shifts and it all balanced itself out. Nothing like offering the car to a seventeen-year-old if they stopped by the store and got a loaf of bread and a gallon on milk on their way home. Eventually it got to the point where I didn’t have to ask them to do anything in exchange for the car or their independence; they offered it up for free.
